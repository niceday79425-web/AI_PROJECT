1. Setting a 'Psychological Goal' for a 1 Million Won Investment

The most important thing to watch out for when starting as a beginner with 1 million won is "impatience." Rather than aiming to become rich overnight, you should focus on "experiencing how the dividend system actually works."

  • Learning Size: 1 million won is the optimal amount to experience market volatility firsthand without losing sleep.
  • Success Experience: Once you experience even the price of a cup of coffee being automatically deposited into your account every month, investing changes from "studying" to "pleasure."
  • System Building: Setting up a solid portfolio structure when your assets are small ensures you won't be shaken even when your assets grow to 100 million or 1 billion won.

3. 3 Practical Tips for Small-scale Investors

① Utilize Fractional Trading to the Fullest (200%)

US blue-chip stocks are often expensive per share ($200 to $400). To buy multiple stocks with 1 million won, fractional trading is essential. Build your own index by diversifying into multiple sectors, even if it's in increments of 0.1 or 0.05 shares.

② Minimize Commissions and Currency Exchange Costs

In a 1 million won investment, a transaction fee of 0.25% is larger than you think. Make sure to check brokerages' "commission discount promotions" and "exchange fee discounts of 90% or more" before opening an account.

③ Automated Recurring Purchases (Automated Investing)

Rather than buying everything at once with 1 million won, create a system to buy 200,000 won worth of shares over 5 months, or add 100,000 won every month. "Recurring investing" is the most powerful tool to exclude human emotion and lower your average cost basis.

4. Roadmap: How 1 Million Won Becomes 100 Million Won

  1. Planting the Seed: Buy your first stock today with 1 million won.
  2. Watering: Reinvest every single dollar of dividends you receive without withdrawing any of it (DRIP).
  3. Fertilizing: Consistently deposit a portion of your salary (e.g., 300,000 won) to thicken your pipeline.
  4. Waiting: Endure the time until the YOC (Yield on Cost) you learned about in Part 6 reaches double digits.
