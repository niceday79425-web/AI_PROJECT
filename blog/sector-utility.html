Following our analyses of consumer goods and healthcare, the sector we are examining today is 'Utilities,' which was once called a 'boring bond proxy' but is now drawing attention as one of the hottest growth sectors in the US stock market.

The utility sector consists of companies providing essential infrastructure services such as electricity, gas, and water. Protected by government regulations in exchange for monopoly status, their revenues are highly stable, which has traditionally classified them as a quintessential high-dividend sector.

However, as of 2025–2026, the utility sector has entered a massive paradigm shift. As the astronomical electricity demand consumed by artificial intelligence (AI) data centers converges with the transition to clean renewable energy, utilities are shifting from 'safe defensive stocks' to 'structural growth stocks.'

Key Takeaway: The utility sector maintains its traditional high dividend (3%–4% range) appeal while serving as the central engine of 'power supply'—the essential infrastructure of the AI era—offering strong stock price momentum (capital appreciation) and making it a must-have investment sector in 2026.

1. Detailed Concept: The Appeal and Evolution of the Utility Sector

Utility stocks are highly sensitive to the macroeconomic environment, particularly interest rates and electricity demand.

📉 Primary Beneficiary of the Rate Stabilization Cycle

Utility companies require heavy debt to build large-scale infrastructure such as power plants, transmission towers, and water mains. Consequently, their stock prices were weighed down by high interest expenses during periods of elevated rates. However, the interest rate cuts that began in late 2024 and have stabilized in 2026 are dramatically reducing financing costs for utility firms, serving as a catalyst to boost dividend capacity.

⚡ AI Data Centers and the Electricity Shortage

Data centers powering generative AI, like ChatGPT, consume tens of times more electricity than traditional data centers. As concerns over power shortages in the US intensify in 2026, the value of utility companies that can reliably supply electricity is skyrocketing. Essentially, a structure has been established where the growth of tech companies directly drives revenue increases for utility firms.

2. Real Data & Cases: Representative US Utility Dividend Stocks

Here are representative US stocks that capture both AI growth momentum and stable dividend income as of 2026.

📈 Leaders in Renewable Energy and AI Growth

  • NextEra Energy (NEE): The world's largest wind and solar generator and the largest utility company in the US. A key partner delivering clean energy to big tech data centers, it has posted around 10% annual dividend growth for decades. It is the premier utility stock combining an attractive mid-3% yield and solid growth.
  • Constellation Energy (CEG): The largest carbon-free energy (nuclear power) producer in the US. Its stock price has risen sharply as big tech companies increasingly favor nuclear power for around-the-clock, uninterrupted data center operations. Although its yield has compressed to under 1% due to rapid price appreciation, it offers stellar capital gains and strong future dividend growth potential.

🛡️ Defensive Stocks with Traditional High Yields and Solid Fundamentals

  • Duke Energy (DUK): A traditional utility powerhouse delivering stable electricity and gas across the eastern United States. Virtually immune to economic cycles, it maintains a high dividend yield exceeding 4%, making it an optimal pick for retirees and high-yield investors.
  • Dominion Energy (D): A utility company based in Virginia, which is known as the 'datacenter capital of the world.' Having finalized its restructuring, it is enjoying an earnings turnaround in 2026 while paying a reliable high yield of around 4.5%.

Risks and Considerations in Utility Investing

  1. Climate Change & Natural Disaster Risks: Large-scale natural disasters such as wildfires or hurricanes can destroy transmission infrastructure and trigger massive liability risks (as seen in the PG&E case). Focus on financially strong, top-tier companies that actively invest in grid modernization.
  2. Rebound in Interest Rates: While interest rates are currently stable in 2026, any potential flare-up in inflation that drives central banks back toward hikes would cause a sharp, temporary correction in the utility sector.
